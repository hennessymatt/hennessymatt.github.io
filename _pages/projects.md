Our research  combines mathematical modelling, mathematical "analysis", and scientific computing to learn about the behaviours of fluids, solids, and multi-component systems with modern applications.  We work closely with experimental researchers.  Our mathematical models are typically based on continuum mechanics.  "Analysing" these models means seeking exact or approximate solutions or learning about the qualitative behaviour of solutions (e.g. using asymptotic methods or tools from dynamical systems).  Our portfolio of research projects is continually evolving, but below you can find a sample of the problems we have been thinking about.

<div class="projects">

<h2 class="category">mechanics of hydrogels</h2>
<div class="row justify-content-sm-center">
    <div class="col-sm-4 mt-3 mt-md-0">
        {% include figure.html path="assets/img/gel_beads.jpg" title="A flask filled with hydrogel beads" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" %}
    </div>
    <div class="col-sm-8 mt-3 mt-md-0">
        <p>
        Hydrogels are soft solids consisting of swollen network of spring-like molecules.  From a mechanical point of view, hydrogels are interesting because their behaviour is dictated by a combination of fluid mechanics, solid mechanics, and thermodynamics. By subjecting hydrogels to stimuli (e.g. an electric field), the degree of swelling and hence the behaviour of the gel can be finely controlled, making them useful for a wide range of applications.  We are currently looking at:
        <ul>
          <li>Phase transitions, instabilities, and pattern formation in hydrogels</li>
          <li>Modelling and simulation of electro-active gels </li>
          <li>Optimisation of hydrogel-based drug delivery systems</li>
          <li>Fibre-reinforced hydrogels for artificial cartilage</li>
          <li>Hydrogel-coated stem cells for liver therapy</li>
        </ul>
        </p>
    </div>
</div>

<h2 class="category">lithium-ion batteries</h2>
<div class="row justify-content-sm-center">
    <div class="col-sm-4 mt-3 mt-md-0">
        {% include figure.html path="assets/img/lib.jpg" title="Lithium-ion batteries" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" %}
    </div>
    <div class="col-sm-8 mt-3 mt-md-0">
        <p>
        Lithium-ion batteries power many of today's portable electronic devices and will play a key role in the future as society moves towards electric vehicles.  Despite the numerous advantages of lithium-ion batteries, they are prone to overheating and long-term degradation.  Moreover, their energy density may be too low for future applications.  The CFM Lab is using mathematical modelling to learn how to overcome these challenges.  We've been thinking about:
        <ul>
          <li>Using asymptotic methods to derive simplified models of batteries that capture thermo-electrochemical processes across multiple scales</li>
          <li>Understanding the thermo-electrochemical impact of a short circuit within a battery pack and how this can be detected before the battery overheats</li>
          <li>Using nanowire-based electrodes to reduce degradation</li>
        </ul>
        </p>
    </div>
</div>

<h2 class="category">light-driven 3D printing</h2>
<div class="row justify-content-sm-center">
    <div class="col-sm-4 mt-3 mt-md-0">
        {% include figure.html path="assets/img/3d_printing.jpg" title="3D printing in action" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" %}
    </div>
    <div class="col-sm-8 mt-3 mt-md-0">
        <p>
        3D printing has revolutionised the rapid and cheap fabrication of complex objects.  At the heart of many 3D-printing technologies is a process called photopolymerisation, in which a photo-sensitive liquid is transformed into a solid upon exposure to light.  Fully harnessing the capabilities of light-driven 3D printing requires mathematical models that capture the wide range of chemistry and physics that occur but which are simple enough to be experimentally calibrated and used in optimisation algorithms.  Our work has focused on:
        <ul>
          <li>Deriving minimal models of photopolymerisation that capture the influence of mass transport, optical attenuation, and thermal effects on the growth of 3D-printed solids.</li>
          <li>Understanding the fluid and solid mechanics of 3D printing and how this can be harnessed to self-assemble complex structures.</li>
        </ul>
        </p>
    </div>
</div>
